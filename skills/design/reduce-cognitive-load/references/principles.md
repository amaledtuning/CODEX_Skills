# Cognitive Load Principles

Source anchor: paraphrased from the design book in `D:\Project Skills\Книги по дизайну\100_glavnyh_principov_dizayna_kak_uderzhat_vnimanie.pdf`, especially working memory, long-term memory, recognition over recall, attention, and low-friction path sections around pp. 60-69, 119-139, and 160-169.

## Core Principles

- Working memory is limited. Do not ask people to hold many facts, steps, codes, or exceptions in mind at once.
- Prefer recognition over recall. Show options, context, examples, summaries, and previous choices instead of forcing memory.
- Chunk related information. Smaller meaningful groups are easier to understand than one large undifferentiated block.
- Reveal complexity progressively. Show advanced detail when it becomes relevant, not before.
- Keep context visible across steps. Users should not need to remember what they chose three screens or slides ago.
- Make the easy path the intended path. People choose low-friction routes, especially when tired or uncertain.
- Reduce context switching. Every switch between pages, tools, modes, or mental models costs attention.
- Support repetition and familiar patterns when learning matters.

## Practical Checklist

- List everything the user must remember to complete the task.
- Remove, automate, or display anything that should not be memorized.
- Split a dense process into named chunks or steps.
- Keep one primary task per screen, slide, or page section when possible.
- Use previews, summaries, breadcrumbs, progress indicators, and persistent selections.
- Replace blank recall fields with searchable lists, examples, suggestions, or defaults.
- Move rare or advanced options behind disclosure controls.
- In diagrams and explainers, introduce one concept layer at a time.
- In dashboards, separate monitoring, diagnosis, and action zones.

## Anti-Patterns

- Long forms that require remembering previous answers.
- Slides that introduce many new concepts at once.
- Instructions that depend on terms not yet explained.
- Settings pages where common and rare options have equal prominence.
- Dashboards that mix status, analysis, and action without hierarchy.
- Hidden dependencies between choices.

## Output Pattern

When using this skill, produce:

1. User goal and current cognitive load sources.
2. What to chunk, remove, defer, automate, or make visible.
3. Recognition cues and context aids.
4. Simplified flow or information sequence.
5. Medium-specific notes for UI, slide, PDF, document, diagram, dashboard, or visual explanation.
